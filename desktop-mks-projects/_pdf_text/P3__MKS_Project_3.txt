1 Introduction
A small-world network is a structure of graphs with nodes connected by edges
in a way that leads to high clustering and short distances. In the example of a
social network where the nodes represent individuals and the edges describe
social connections, high clustering would imply that if two people share a
common friend, they are likely to know each other as well.
The Watts-Strogatz model is a model which produces random undirected
graphs, where nodes are linked symmetrically, with small-world properties.
A Markov chain is a stochastic process that describes transitions between
states where the probability of every next state is determined by current
states.
In this project, small world networks with graphs generated by the Watts
Strogatz model were studied. In parallel, the convergence speed of a Markov
chain in relation to the eigenvalues of its transition matrix was analysed.

2.5 Interpretation
For the Watts–Strogatz graphs, the most important observation is that the
average path length decreases much faster than the clustering coefficient.
The clearest small-world range in this experiment is approximately between
p= 0.01 andp= 0.1. In this range, the graph still has relatively high clus-
tering, but the average path length is already much smaller than atp= 0.
For instance, atp= 0.03, clustering is still about 92% of its value atp= 0,
while the average path length is only about 37% of its value atp= 0.
The results also show that one graph realization would not be enough to
describe the small-pregime reliably. For very smallp, shortcuts are rare,
so different realizations can have noticeably different average path lengths
and diameters. This is why averaging over many realizations is important.
In contrast, the average degree is not variable, because the Watts–Strogatz
rewiring procedure preserves the number of edges.

2.6 Conclusion
The Watts–Strogatz simulations show the small-world effect: a small amount
of rewiring can strongly reduce the average path length while preserving much
of the clustering. The strongest evidence appears for small positive rewiring
probabilities such asp= 0.01 andp= 0.03.

3.6 Conclusion
When choosing initial conditions for studying Markov-chains, using only one
initial condition could miss a slower-decaying component. The obvious ex-
ample of this would be if the initial state was exactly or very close to the, the
stationary distribution. Another example would be if the initial configuration
was orthogonal to the slowest decaying eigenvector.
4 Discussion points
1. Our observations show that for our chosen paramaters of N and k, the
value of p that give the most high clustering together with short average
path length occurs approximately for
0.01≤p≤0.1.
This we can conclude this from examening our data that shows that in
this interval of values of p we have that:
The clustering is still high,
C(0)
C(p) ≈[0.745,0.972]
The path length is low and repidly decreasing,
ℓ(0)
ℓ(p) ≈[0.259,0.560]
This quantitative gap is the signature of the small-world effect. Where
the clustering, describing the local structure is only mildly effected
while global distances decrease rapidly. This shows how a small number
of rewired edges introduces long-range shortcuts in stochastic graph
models.
2. We observed that the variability of the distance statistics was largest
for small positivep. This is reasonable because for very small rewiring
probabilities, the number and placement of shortcuts vary significantly
between realizations. Oncepis larger, many shortcuts are present and
the average path length becomes more stable across realizations.
16

===== PAGE 18 =====
We can tie this to probability theory. Where in our situation for larger
p, many shortcuts exist simultaneously and the law of large numbers
makes the graph statistics more stable across realizations. While for
smaller p, individual shortcuts matter enormously, creating less stabil-
ity and higher variation.
3. As stated previously, the Markov-chain simulations show that conver-
gence to stationarity is controlled by the second-largest eigenvalue mod-
ulus. The numerical results agree with the spectral argument. The base
matrixP 0 has the smallestµand the largest spectral gap, and it reaches
total variation distance below 10−3 in the fewest steps. The lazy matrix
P0.8 hasµclosest to one and the smallest spectral gap, and it converges
the slowest.
4. The spectral gap is a more direct predictor of convergence speed than
visual inspection of the matrix entries because convergence depends on
how powers ofPact on error vectors. This action is described by the
eigenvalues. Matrix entries describe one-step transition probabilities,
but they do not directly show how quickly all non-stationary compo-
nents decay under repeated multiplication.
5. There are also limitations. In the network part, using only one realiza-
tion for eachpcould lead to misleading conclusions, especially at small
p. In the Markov-chain part, using only one initial condition could
miss a slower-decaying component. For this reason, I used 1000 graph
realizations for eachpand all four concentrated initial states for the
Markov chain. Still, the numerical results are empirical observations,
while the eigenvalue estimate is a mathematical explanation under the
stated assumptions.
